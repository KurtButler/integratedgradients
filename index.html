Explainable AI and Feature Attribution

Explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) studies how we can make model behavior easier to inspect, justify, and trust. A central idea is feature attribution: decomposing a prediction into contributions from the input variables so that we can better understand why a model produced a result.

We are especially interested in mathematically grounded attribution methods that do more than assign a single score per feature. The two works below explore richer directions: one studies attribution under Gaussian process uncertainty, and the other extends attribution to higher-order feature interactions.

In the simplest view, we would like explanations of the form φ1(x) + ··· + φd(x) ≈ f(x) - f(x′), but these papers ask what happens when uncertainty or multi-feature interactions matter as well.

Higher-Order Feature Attribution: Bridging Statistics, Explainable AI, and Topological Signal Processing

Kurt Butler, Guanchao Feng, and Petar M. Djurić (2025 / ICASSP 2026)

  • arXiv
  • Official publication

This work asks how attribution should behave when predictions depend on interactions between several variables rather than on isolated features. Instead of stopping at first-order contributions, it develops a theory of higher-order attribution built on integrated gradients.

The paper is exciting from an XAI perspective because it connects attribution theory with ideas from statistics and topological signal processing. In practical terms, it moves explanation beyond “which feature mattered most?” toward “which combinations of features created the effect, and how are those interactions structured?”

Explainable Learning with Gaussian Processes

Kurt Butler, Guanchao Feng, and Petar M. Djurić (2024)

  • arXiv
  • Code

This paper studies attribution for Gaussian process regression (GPR), where the model prediction is not just flexible but also explicitly uncertain. The key contribution is a principled definition of feature attributions under model uncertainty, together with closed-form expressions that stay interpretable.

A particularly attractive result is that when integrated gradients are used as the attribution method, the attributions themselves inherit a Gaussian process structure. That means the explanation comes with uncertainty estimates, which is valuable when the model is making predictions in data-sparse or extrapolative settings.
